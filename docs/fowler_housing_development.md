# Fowler Housing Development — Reference Brief

Email: comments@bozeman.net | Subject: "Public Comment on Fowler Avenue Housing Development"

## What Is Being Proposed

The City proposes annexing and zoning ~5 acres of a 10-acre city-owned parcel (Geocode: 06-0798-02-3-01-05-0000) as R-3 high-density housing along Fowler Avenue between Oak and Annie Streets. The remaining ~5 acres is for the Fowler Avenue Connector road.

- Up to 84 units at ~18 units/acre with 168 cars
- Buildings up to 4-5 stories
- Unit prices: $450K–$650K (not affordable by most metrics)
- City purchased land in 2017; agreed in purchase memorandum to "preserve existing trees, open spaces and irrigation ditch"

## Why the Current Proposal Is Wrong

### Site Is Functionally Undevelopable for High Density

- Parcel is only 150 feet wide
- After 35-foot setbacks: 80 feet of buildable width
- After 25-foot road + 25-foot driveways: only 30 feet for structures
- Current proposal reduces setbacks to 20 feet and uses single-lane alleyway — still infeasible
- Alleyway dead-ends make most units inaccessible to emergency/ladder trucks

### Impacts on 21 Adjacent Properties

- 18 homeowners have backyards directly bordering the parcel
- No roadway buffer (unlike other R-3 to R-1 transitions in Bozeman)
- ~50% of surface parking and alleyway run along existing backyards
- Anticipated property value loss: ~$100K per home ($2.1M+ across adjacent properties, $5M+ broader neighborhood)
- Noise, light pollution, vehicle emissions from parking/alleyways at property lines
- 6-foot privacy fence is inadequate mitigation

### Infrastructure and Traffic

- Entrances on Farmall and Caterpillar Streets (residential roads not designed for high-volume traffic)
- No access planned from Fowler Avenue itself (the arterial road)
- High-density traffic substantially increases loads on New Holland Street
- Parking ratio of 1.67 vehicles/unit is inadequate; garages likely used for storage
- Overflow parking will spill into Harvest Creek streets

### Not Actually Affordable

- $450K–$650K units don't qualify as affordable housing
- No grants, sponsorships, or nonprofit collaboration (HRDC, Trust Montana) mentioned
- Other R-3 affordable developments already underway (160 acres at Baxter/Cottonwood)
- Design models from major metro areas (SLC, Seattle, Manhattan) are incompatible with Bozeman

### Inadequate Public Process

- Fast-tracked without sufficient public input
- No outreach via mail, email, or door-to-door canvassing
- City website offers minimal information — no contacts, timelines, or engagement methods
- Pre-zoning and long-range annexation planning (standard best practices) were not followed

## Fowler Avenue Connector Road Context

The road project and housing are interdependent. Infrastructure must be aligned.

- 2-lane minor arterial: Oak to Huffine Lane (3 phases, 2026-2028)
- 10-foot shared-use paths both sides, roundabout at Annie/Durston, traffic signal at Babcock
- 60% design reduced Harvest Creek buffer from 30 feet to 15 feet
- Development should be planned in direct coordination with road construction

## What Neighbors Want (HCHOA Position)

1. **Density**: Max 8 units/acre, no more than 2 dwelling units per building, 1-2 stories only
2. **Greenspace**: At least 50% (per Sippel Trust purchase agreement and Montana Constitution Art. II §3)
3. **Traffic**: No road connections to Harvest Creek; entrances on Fowler Ave only; bike/ped paths by HOA agreement
4. **Setbacks**: No roads or parking against existing backyards, even with 20-foot setback
5. **Parking**: 2 spaces/unit minimum, carports not garages
6. **Process**: 3-5 year planning horizon; secure grants/sponsorships; consensus process covenant recorded before any action
7. **Model**: Bridger View — 1-2 story single-family/duplex, ~50% greenspace, ADA-compliant units

## Consensus Process

On January 6, 2026, after months of citizen outcry, the City Commission invited Harvest Creek HOA to participate in a "consensus-based engagement" for the housing development. Preliminary mediation interviews are underway. Any separate zoning actions (like the Hanson Lane annexation) risk undermining this process.
