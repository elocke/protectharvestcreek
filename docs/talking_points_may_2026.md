# Talking Points — May 5, 2026 Commission Hearing on Hanson Lane Annexation #25-775

These are sample talking points circulated by Harvest Creek HOA ahead of the May 5, 2026 City Commission vote (Agenda item N) on the Hanson Lane Annexation. The goal is denial or delay of Application #25-775 in favor of gradual intensification of development, with the parcel zoned R-A rather than R-B.

When generating a comment, draw on the specific facts, names, and arguments below. Do not copy whole sentences — each comment should be in the resident's own voice, with a unique opener and a different mix of arguments than the last comment generated.

## Core Ask

Deny or delay Application #25-775. Direct staff to consider R-A zoning, which is consistent with the City's growth policy, the future land use map, and the actual development pattern of the surrounding neighborhood.

## Argument Bank A — Surrounding Land Use Doesn't Support R-B

The Harvest Creek subdivision is immediately adjacent to the subject property. Neighboring properties are single-household residential. That is the dominant use of land in the surrounding area, not multi-family residential. The facts on the ground cannot be changed and should not be ignored.

R-B allows density greater than 6 units per acre and buildings taller than 40 feet. That is not consistent with the development pattern of the surrounding area.

The annexation is needed to install the Fowler Avenue right-of-way. The Connector will join Annie Street from Harvest Creek to its dead-end on the western boundary of the parcel. The City's plan describes a roundabout at Fowler and Annie. Without traffic calming, Annie Street becomes an unobstructed raceway to Emily Dickinson Elementary School, which serves the neighborhoods west of the subject property. No annexation should be approved without definitive conditions for speed limits and installed traffic calming. Recent local experience shows crosswalks paired with roundabouts are a problem. Have the impact on young pedestrians evaluated before deciding.

## Argument Bank B — No Transit, No Services, No Justification for R-B

The area between Oak and Durston along the proposed Fowler right-of-way has neither good access to transit nor proximity to community services such as shopping or medical. R-B is appropriate for areas with good access to parks, community services, and/or transit.

The closest shopping area is over a mile east — a 30-minute walk both ways. Public transit in the area is limited. There is a park nearby, which is the only qualifying factor. The total size of this parcel, once the right-of-way is built, will be 5 acres or less. Surrounding density is 6 units per acre or less, equivalent to R-A. There is no area in the vicinity that would fit the description of an R-B use.

It is well known in the neighborhood that the City desires lands in this area for high-density, high-rise housing. Within the past 10 months, the City has made several public presentations of just such a plan for the land immediately next to the subject property. This zoning decision will affect both properties.

R-A conforms with the Urban Neighborhood designation in the Community Plan. The Urban Neighborhood designation is a policy statement, not a regulation. R-A allows enough diversity of housing options to satisfy that designation.

## Argument Bank C — R-A Matches Goal N.1.11 of the Community Plan

R-A zoning is consistent with the Urban Neighborhood designation in the Bozeman Community Plan 2025 (BCP). There are significant distinctions between R-A and R-B districts — among them density and allowed building heights. Those distinctions have been described to the Commission in written public comment.

Community Plan 2025 Goal N.1.11 states the Urban Neighborhood category is to "enable gradual and predictable density in developed areas over time." Under the new BCP, R-A "is designed to hold multiple types of housing on a variety of lot sizes."

R-B will not achieve gradual, predictable increase in density because of the surrounding R-1 and R-2 uses, which are already built.

This zoning change will determine the zoning assigned to the remaining unincorporated, developed and undeveloped parcels in this wholly enclosed land. If R-B is ordered, additional acres of land could be developed at that same density. On April 14, 2026, in open session of the Bozeman City Commission, both Deputy Mayor Douglas Fischer and City Manager Chuck Winn pledged that no 4- and 5-story development will occur on a parcel immediately adjacent to this one. The development challenges are the same for both parcels. The outcomes should be the same.

## Argument Bank D — Heights and the New UDC

No buildings in the area of the subject property are higher than 2 stories.

Under the newly adopted UDC 2025, an R-A zone combines the attributes of the former R-1 and R-2 zones: a range of buildings from single-family residences to two-family residences, one and two stories tall, capping building heights at 40 feet. As requested by the applicant, R-B will allow five 5-story buildings with 8 dwelling units per acre to be built as of right, with only the issuance of a building permit.

The Urban Neighborhood designation has the greatest variety of assigned zoning districts of any designation in the BCP — seven different zoning districts. R-A satisfies the BCP criteria for the gradual introduction of densification. It was made for this type of annexation and zoning map change.

## Argument Bank E — The Volume and Nature of Public Comment

More than 90 public comments have already been submitted on this matter. That is a significant number, and it amply demonstrates that R-B will be disruptive of the existing neighborhoods adjoining the subject parcel.

Significant community impact has worked a change in the design of the Fowler Connection between Durston and Oak, such that it is not suited for high-intensity development. All community input on the road design assumed those lands would remain zoned SRR or at least low-density residential.

Traffic congestion and speeds will increase on roads not designed for the increased traffic. Have this evaluated before making the zoning decision. Vehicles will proliferate and parking will be strained. There is no easy public transportation that competes with car ownership, and there is no proximity to services. Parking maintenance and restrictions in the Harvest Creek covenants are unenforceable against non-residents. Overflow parking and traffic will spill into Harvest Creek and place burdens on those property owners.

## Three Projects on the 20-Acre Field West of New Holland Drive

For context — three separate projects are planned:

1. **Fowler Housing Development**: Phase 2 of the consensus process begins mid-May and currently includes only four known Harvest Creek homeowners.
2. **Fowler Avenue Connector**: Three segments, each with its own budget and construction timeline. The northern segment from Oak Street to Durston Road is at 90% design and construction is expected to start summer 2026.
3. **Hanson Lane Annexation and Zoning**: 10.03-acre parcel acquired as part of the Connector. After the road, the landowner is seeking R-B. Harvest Creek is asking for R-A because of lower height limits and lower density.

The City Commission will vote on the Hanson Lane annexation and zoning in back-to-back votes on May 5. The Community Development Board has recommended approval despite the neighborhood sending in over 84 written comments and 12 oral comments at the April 20 hearing. The comment window closes at noon on Tuesday, May 5, 2026. The meeting itself is at 6 PM at City Hall, 121 North Rouse — Hanson Lane is Agenda item N.
